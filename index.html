How does Crohn's disease begin?

We study the earliest immunological events in Crohn's disease, focusing on gut-associated lymphoid tissue and the cellular mechanisms that precede overt inflammation.

Research overview

Crohn's disease is a chronic inflammatory bowel disease with a poorly understood aetiology. Our work centres on gut-associated lymphoid tissue (GALT) — particularly Peyer's patches and isolated lymphoid follicles — as the probable sites of disease initiation. We investigate how immune complex signalling on mononuclear phagocytes at the subepithelial dome may act as a trigger for the earliest pathological events, including the formation of aphthoid ulcers.

We combine human FFPE tissue analysis, multiplex immunofluorescence, intestinal organoid co-culture, and computational histopathology to understand the follicle-level immune architecture that distinguishes health from early disease.

01

GALT and disease initiation

Characterising the immune microenvironment of Peyer's patches and isolated lymphoid follicles in human Crohn's disease resection tissue.

02

Mononuclear phagocyte biology

Mapping macrophage subsets and FcγR expression at the subepithelial dome; understanding how immune complex signalling drives early inflammation.

03

Multiplex immunofluorescence

Developing high-dimensional mIF panels for FFPE human tissue to resolve cellular identity, activation state, and spatial organisation within follicles.

04

Computational histopathology

Building image analysis pipelines in QuPath for automated annotation, cell classification, and spatial quantification of GALT histology.

05

Organoid co-culture

Modelling M-cell and epithelial-immune interactions using intestinal organoids co-cultured with immune cells, including whole-mount imaging approaches.

06

In vivo modelling

Transgenic and gnotobiotic mouse models to probe AIEC colonisation of M cells and GALT-driven ileitis as a model of aphthoid ulcer initiation.
